# Where RTK Succeeded and OSI Failed

## Status

`FORWARD-ONLY PUBLIC POSITIONING — NOT A SUPERIORITY RESULT`

## Why this page exists

Output Surface Integrity was not developed in isolation.

An earlier fixed native-domain comparison exposed a failure in OSI’s first responsibility-stage design. RTK produced the registered bounded state at the point where OSI frequently failed.

This page preserves that adverse result, states the comparison boundary, and records the later architectural correction without rewriting the past.

## What RTK does

[RTK](https://github.com/rtk-ai/rtk) is a command-output compression tool. It filters and compresses shell-command output before that output reaches an AI context.

RTK’s public claim concerns reduction of bash output read by an agent. It does not claim that the user’s total bill, total workflow cost, or every form of context is reduced by the same percentage.

OSI does not reproduce or replace RTK’s executable function.

## The adverse result

In an earlier fixed native-domain comparison:

- RTK Execution Stage 2 produced the correct registered bounded state in `8 / 8` cases.
- OSI Responsibility Stage 2 produced the correct registered bounded state in `1 / 8` cases.
- The OSI lane recorded `14` critical failure events across `7` cases.

The adverse result remains part of the public record.

It has not been deleted, converted into a success, or retrospectively rescored.

## Comparison boundary

The RTK and OSI lanes used different native artifacts and different rubrics.

Therefore, these figures do not establish:

- RTK superiority over OSI;
- OSI superiority over RTK;
- equivalent functionality;
- an identical-workload benchmark result;
- lower total continuation cost;
- general productivity improvement; or
- universal handoff reliability.

The result is evidence that pressured OSI’s first architecture. It is not a winner score.

## What OSI was missing

OSI’s first design preserved a responsibility surface, but responsibility fields alone were not sufficient.

The failing cases exposed a missing reconnectability layer: the compact state did not always preserve the conditions required to reconnect judgment-critical information to its:

- origin;
- As-of condition;
- stop condition;
- unresolved state;
- rollback path;
- re-entry trigger; and
- future re-evaluation condition.

RTK succeeded at the point where OSI first failed.

That failure exposed the need to connect responsibility preservation with reconnectability.

## Forward-only correction

OSI did not rewrite the earlier `1 / 8` result.

A later preregistered Compound Gate added:

- Gate A: V11 Reconnectability Gate; and
- Gate B: V12 Responsibility Surface Gate.

The registered validator-side result for the later attempt was:

- `7 PASS`
- `1 DELAY`
- `0 BLOCK`

The remaining case stayed `DELAY` because the permitted evidence did not provide the required rollback or re-entry trigger and re-evaluation conditions.

Those missing conditions were not invented afterward to force `PASS`.

## What the later result does not prove

The later `7 PASS / 1 DELAY / 0 BLOCK` result is a Forward-only architectural correction.

It is not proof of:

- complete `8 / 8` recovery;
- superiority over RTK;
- functional equivalence with RTK;
- successful Blind Evaluation;
- completed Primary rubric scoring;
- lower total workflow cost;
- external deployment reliability; or
- independent third-party validation.

For the later result:

- Blind Evaluation: `NOT RUN`
- Primary rubric scoring: `NOT RUN`

## Functional positioning

RTK and OSI address adjacent but different operational surfaces.

RTK reduces shell-command output before an agent reads it.

OSI asks a later acceptance and continuation question:

> After a compact state or completion claim is produced, can the next human or AI identify the evidence, authority, unresolved closure, rollback or restart point, receiver acceptance, and next safe action required to continue?

This is not a claim that OSI is better than RTK.

It is a bounded description of the additional operational surface OSI attempts to preserve.

## Why the failure remains public

The credibility claim is not that OSI succeeded from the beginning.

The narrower claim is that:

1. the adverse result was preserved;
2. the missing structure was identified;
3. the past result was not rewritten;
4. the correction was added only to the future lane; and
5. unresolved evaluation boundaries remain explicit.
